Barakoa

Barakoa is a cultural inquiry exploring the masks people wear around love, fear, and silence ... and what happens when those masks are exposed, challenged, or held gently.

It asks questions about protection and vulnerability, performance and truth, what we reveal, and what we hide.

Barakoa is not just an event. It is a system of sound, body, narrative, and encounter.

How Fabri Enters Barakoa

Fabri enters Barakoa as the practice responsible for material translation.

Barakoa speaks about love, fear, silence, and the masks we carry. Fabri listens, then responds through making — shaping those tensions into things that are worn, moved in, and felt.

Fashion becomes the primary language because it sits closest to the body. It is where masking is practiced daily: in posture, in exposure, in protection, in choice.

Through form, texture, and movement, Fabri turns Barakoa’s questions into physical presence as statement.

The Barakoa Aesthetic Theme: NiloFunk

NiloFunk is the aesthetic language guiding Barakoa’s fashion and styling.

It is a fusion of:

  • Nilo — Nilotic cultural references (across Nile communities) with their rich textile histories, body adornment practices, and ceremonial dress traditions.
  • Funk — the bold, expressive styles of 1970s and 80s African American culture, characterised by vibrant colors, eclectic patterns, and a spirit of individuality and rebellion.
  • Contemporary — modern reinterpretations of both Nilo and Funk aesthetics, blending traditional elements with current design sensibilities.

Masking as Design Logic

Barakoa’s six anchor masks operate as behavioural archetypes — not costumes, but embodied behaviours.

Fabri translates these through silhouette, restriction versus freedom, exposure versus concealment, weight, texture, and flow.
