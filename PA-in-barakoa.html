Barakoa

Barakoa is a world built around masking.

It starts from a simple observation:

that human beings rarely move through life as they are. Instead, we learn to adapt, perform, protect, and manage how we appear in order to survive.

In Barakoa, masks are not costumes.

They are systems of behavior.

They determine how people speak, how they move, how they respond to fear, and how they relate to others. Over time, these masks stop being temporary. They become habitual. Eventually, they replace presence.

Barakoa is not interested in asking why people mask.

  • It is interested in what masking makes possible,
  • what kinds of relationships masking enables,
  • and what kinds of responsibility masking makes easy to avoid.

This is the terrain into which Perpetrators Anonymous enters.

How Perpetrators Anonymous Enters Barakoa

Perpetrators Anonymous (PA) enters Barakoa through the play.

This is how PA participates.

PA is a survivor-centered organization that addresses harm and violence by working with remorseful perpetrators to take accountability, heal, and change their ways—while also advocating for systems and policies that ensure justice.

Barakoa asks what it means to wear a mask.

PA responds by staging what happens beneath the mask.

This play is PA’s creative method of holding its central inquiry for the year:

understanding and humanizing remorseful perpetrators—without excusing harm, without abandoning justice.

Not to explain perpetrators.

Not to defend them.

But to make visible the human processes that make harm possible—and interruptible.

The Barakoa Play: Love, Fear, and the Quiet

[ PLAY OPERATING SYSTEM ]

What This Play Is

Barakoa: Love, Fear, and the Quiet is a live theatrical work staged inside a Barakoa gig. It is not only a story — it is a living system that the audience enters, observes, and moves alongside.

The play explores how a human being learns to survive the world by putting on different masks. These masks help us cope with pressure, expectation, danger, and intimacy. Over time, however, the same masks that protect us can also separate us from ourselves.

At its core, the play asks:

  • What does love look like when it is real?
  • What happens to fear when it is managed instead of felt?
  • What becomes of quiet in a world that never stops?

The play is reflective and intimate, with moments of dry humor and calm directness. Its stylistic influence comes from works like The Wonderful Story of Henry Sugar — clear narration, steady pacing, and a voice that calmly explains.

The Central Story

The story follows Mwanadamu (MD) — a word that simply means human being. MD moves through the world carrying a wooden backpack known as the Barakoa Bag(B-Bag).

Inside the bag are masks. Each mask represents a way of surviving specific situations — at work, in love, in conflict, or in public life.

Over time:

  • • The masks are used more often
  • • Multiple masks operate at once
  • • The human underneath becomes harder to access

The play does not judge this process. It shows it.

THE BARAKOA WORLD

Barakoa is a world built around masking. It starts from a simple observation: human beings rarely move through life as they are.

In Barakoa, masks are not costumes. They are systems of behavior. They determine how we respond to fear and relate to others.

Inquiry of 2026

  • • What masking enables
  • • Relationships of performance
  • • The ease of avoiding responsibility

The Rules of the Barakoa World

These rules apply to all performances, adaptations, and future stagings of the play.

Mwanadamu (MD)

  • MD represents the unmasked human state
  • MD is slow, vulnerable, and real
  • MD can only exist when no mask is active

When a mask is worn, MD is not present.

The Masks

Masks are not moods or traits; they are full identity systems. When active, they take control of the body and voice.

  • Multiple masks can operate at once
  • The human presence disappears completely
  • The system becomes efficient but unreachable
  • Masks never acknowledge MD.

Love, Fear, and the Quiet

There are two versions of these states:

Real Love, Fear, and Quiet:

  • MD is present
  • MD can hear Security
  • Genuine interaction is possible

Illusions of Love, Fear, and Quiet:

  • Masks dominate
  • Security fades into the background
  • Everything feels resolved, but nothing truly is

This distinction is the emotional engine of the play.

The Barakoa Bag (B-Bag)

The B-Bag is a wooden backpack carried by MD. Inside the bag, are six masks that MD puts on to survive different situations.

It represents:

  • accumulated survival strategies
  • learned behavior
  • Represents inherited coping mechanisms

When the bag is abandoned, it does not disappear. It waits to be picked up again.

Security (Narrator)

Security is the narrator of the story. It is a constant presence, always watching, always listening.

It is the voice that explains the story of the play as it unfolds.

When the bag is abandoned, it does not disappear. It waits to be picked up again.

The Six Masks

There are only six masks in the world of Barakoa.
Each mask is a full identity system, a strategy for survival.

The Keeper

  • Survives by control and responsibility
  • Carries everything “just in case”
  • Believes prevention equals safety

The Silencer

  • Survives by avoiding conflict
  • Reduces conflict by reducing expression
  • Values peace over truth

The Ghost

  • Survives by simply disappearing
  • Withdraws, disappears, avoids
  • Lets time pass instead of engaging

The Justifier

  • Survives by making excuses
  • Shifts blame to avoid responsibility
  • Believes intentions outweigh impact
  • Makes harm sound logical

The Saviour

  • Survives by being needed
  • Creates dependency to stay relevant
  • Feels valuable only when useful

    • The Mirror

    • Survives by reflecting others
    • Sees through the lens of others' expectations
    • Offers clarity without comfort

    Stylistic Reference: The Wonderful Story of Henry Sugar

    The play draws tonal influence from The Wonderful Story of Henry Sugar in method, not story.

    It is an elegant tale of obsession, discipline, and the moral implications of acquiring extraordinary wealth and knowledge. In Henry Sugar, events are explained calmly by the narrator as they unfold.

    The Narrator speaks the most. Actors don't speak constantly...just in select scenes.

    We loved this.

    The steady, instructional voice creates tension by remaining composed even as the implications deepen.

    STRUCTURE OF THE PLAY

    Barakoa: Love, Fear, and the Quiet

    Total duration: approximately 80 minutes 3 Acts one continuous performance, embedded intermission

    Structure: four acts, multiple scenes per act

    The play follows a clear psychological and narrative progression.

    Each act answers a different question about masking, responsibility, and presence.

    ACT I — ORIENTATION

    Sleep, waking, and first use of masks

    Duration: ~20 minutes

    The play begins with Mwanadamu (MD) asleep.

    This opening state establishes the baseline of the human before masking:

    • the body is unguarded
    • there is no performance
    • no role is being managed

    MD is physically present but not yet engaged with the world.

    The Barakoa Bag (B-Bag) is visible from the start. It is closed. Its presence is neutral — neither threatening nor symbolic yet.

    Sound and light enter gradually. The environment begins to demand response.

    This act introduces:

    • the rules of the world
    • the logic of masking
    • the role of Security

    The voice of Security appears during this act. Security explains what the audience is witnessing and occasionally gives simple instructions (where to look, when to notice stillness, when to stay quiet).

    Pressure appears in ordinary forms: expectation, responsibility, discomfort, social demand.

    MD begins to wake and respond. Three masks are introduced in Act I. They are not presented as villains. They are practical solutions to real problems.

    Each mask:

    • changes how MD moves
    • alters posture, pace, and attention
    • makes interaction easier

    By the end of Act I, the audience understands: masking is functional, masking is learned, masking helps people survive.

    The act ends with MD awake, operating, and beginning to rely on the system.

    ACT II — ESCALATION

    When coping becomes dominance

    Duration: ~35 minutes

    Act II is the longest and most complex section of the play. Masking increases in frequency and intensity. MD appears less and less. When a mask is active, MD is not present.

    Additional masks are introduced gradually. They do not arrive all at once. Scenes in this act include:

    • single masks operating alone
    • two or three masks overlapping
    • moments where multiple masks are active simultaneously

    When multiple masks are active: they may speak to each other, interrupt one another, reinforce one another. At no point do masks acknowledge MD.

    The system becomes efficient. Decisions are fast. Responses are smooth.

    In this act:

    • love becomes functional
    • fear is managed instead of felt
    • quiet is controlled rather than inhabited

    Security becomes more active and precise. Security names patterns as they happen: when explanation replaces accountability, when control replaces care, when responsibility is avoided.

    Security may also instruct the audience more directly, heightening awareness rather than emotion.

    Act II does not build toward loud chaos. It builds toward saturation. Everything works. Nothing is resolved.

    ACT III — AFTERMATH

    Exposure without resolution

    Duration: ~25 minutes

    Act III explores the consequences of automation, following Mwanadamu (MD) as they move through a world where masking has become an unconscious, reflexive process.

    The act is structured as a psychological loop, emphasizing the repetitive and exhausting nature of a life lived through personas. It begins in a public social space where MD is entirely absent. One or more masks are active, allowing the body to navigate the environment with smooth, controlled efficiency. To the outside world, nothing appears wrong.

    MD leaves this space and moves through a transitional area. The body continues to move with purpose, but there is no interaction. The masks remain fully engaged.

    This leads to the recurring anchor of the loop: the restroom. In this private space, the masks momentarily drop. MD becomes briefly present, washing their hands and staring at their reflection in the mirror. There is no dialogue, no grand realization, and no decision—it is simply a short, raw return of awareness before the cycle resumes.

    As MD leaves the restroom, the world has shifted. They emerge into a different social setting with new triggers, requiring a different mask. This is the loop in motion: each exit from the restroom (or similar liminal space) resets the stage, forcing MD into a new performance.

    MD moves through several environments in quick succession. Each shift triggers a different combination of masks. Sometimes one is active; sometimes two or three operate simultaneously. The system functions without visible distress, but MD remains absent from their own life.

    Eventually, the pace slows as the environment quietens. Mask activity reduces, not by choice, but because the system has reached its limit. Mwanadamu is tired.

    • Near the end of the act, MD is exhausted, helpless, and fully exposed
    • The B-bag has become a heavy, physical burden
    • MD is found crouching, overwhelmed by the weight of the roles

    The act concludes when MD finally decides to take off the B-bag. They leave it on the ground and go home… to bed.

    ACT IV — CONTINUATION (THE SYSTEM)

    The cycle persists

    This act is quiet. There is no escalation. There is no explanation. Only continuation.

    The stage is divided into two visible spaces. They exist at the same time.

    LEFT SIDE — REST

    On one side, Mwanadamu is asleep. Not collapsed. Not unconscious. Simply sleeping. Their body is still. Breath is slow. The noise of the world does not reach them here. This is not peace. It is pause. The Barakoa Bag is no longer on their back. Security does not speak to this Mwanadamu. Security lets them rest.

    RIGHT SIDE — BEGINNING AGAIN

    On the other side, another Mwanadamu enters. This is not the same person — but they are not different either. They move the same way. They hesitate the same way. They are unmasked. They notice the Barakoa Bag. They stop. They look at it. There is no warning. There is no instruction. The bag is simply there. After a moment, they pick it up. The weight is felt immediately.

    Security returns, speaking to the audience, not loudly, not urgently. Security explains what is happening without judgment:

    • the system does not end
    • the masks remain available
    • rest does not remove the structure

    Security does not speak to stop the process. Security names it.
